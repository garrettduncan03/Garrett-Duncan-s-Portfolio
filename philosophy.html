Education is a process; it is not defined by a single moment of achievement. Learning does not always happen in an instant, and meaningful growth often requires time and patience. While not always easy, patience is one of the most important traits of an educator. As an educator, it is my responsibility to recognize that every student that I come across develops at their own pace and to create an environment where that growth is supported. Patience is not just a virtue in teaching… It is a necessity. By keeping a patient mindset and demeanor, I aim to better meet students where they currently are and set goals and guidelines for where they should be in the future.

A central part of my teaching philosophy is the importance of building strong relationships with students. Building these relationships helps create a classroom environment where students feel respected, valued, and heard. Throughout my life, I have always valued making strong relationships with everyone I have met, whether it was in sports, school, or work. As people build strong relationships with each other, they get more comfortable around each other. Students feeling comfortable in the classroom is essential because they are much more likely to engage with the material and take risks in the classroom. Establishing this environment takes a lot of time and effort, but by being engaged with them in hallways and asking questions about what their interests are outside of school, a relationship will inevitably form.

In addition to relationships, I like to make sure that my classroom is entertaining, fun, and enjoyable for my students. Learning should never feel like it's a chore, but rather an opportunity. It is my role as an educator to design lessons that are meaningful, entertaining, and engaging. I aim to achieve this goal by making my assignments in the classroom relevant to my students' lives, and by giving them multiple forms of instruction. By creating an engaged classroom, it is my hope that when students leave my classroom, their curiosity continues so they can become lifelong learners.

As touched on previously, assessment is another main component of my teaching philosophy. From my time with children, I have found that there is truly no single form of assessment that can fully gauge a student's knowledge in a subject area. This is because students have diverse strengths and weaknesses. In order to actively combat this, I make sure to incorporate not only a variety of assessment methods in my classroom, but also a multitude of instructional strategies. These assessment methods include but are not limited to traditional tests and quizzes, as well as creative assignments such as poster projects, presentations, and collaborative work. By giving these opportunities to students, I can provide more opportunities for students with strengths in certain areas by allowing them to show off their individual talents.

Ultimately, as I spend more time around students in the classroom, it becomes more apparent to me that teaching is not my profession; it is my calling. Helping others achieve their goals and watching them do so is deeply meaningful to me, which gives purpose to my work. I will always aim to stay committed to improving my instruction and adapting my methods to fit the needs of all children. All in all, I am committed to creating a classroom where all students feel comfortable and can succeed. I plan on creating this environment through patience, building strong relationships, having engaged instruction, and diverse assessment.
